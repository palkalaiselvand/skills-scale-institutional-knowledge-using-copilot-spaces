## Overview of OctoAcme Project Management Processes

OctoAcme follows a structured, iterative project management approach designed to deliver customer value reliably and transparently. Projects move through five core phases — Initiation, Planning, Execution, Release, and Retrospective — with clear decision gates, defined roles, and consistent communication rhythms at each stage. The framework emphasizes small, testable increments, data-informed decisions, and psychological safety so teams can surface issues early and improve continuously.

Roles and responsibilities are clearly defined to minimize ambiguity and single-person dependency risk. The core roles include the Project Manager (PM), who coordinates delivery and communication; the Product Manager (PdM), who owns the product vision and backlog prioritization; Developers, who design and implement features; QA/Testing specialists, who validate quality; and Stakeholders, who provide inputs and approvals. Supporting roles such as Technical Lead, Security Engineer, UX/Design Lead, and Scrum Master further strengthen the team's ability to execute with clarity and accountability.

Risk management and communication are treated as first-class concerns. Teams maintain a living Risk Register updated weekly, follow structured escalation paths (Team → PM → Product Lead → Sponsor), and use a single source of truth for project status. Stakeholder communication follows a regular cadence — weekly syncs, milestone-based updates, and ad-hoc escalations — ensuring that all parties remain informed and aligned throughout the project lifecycle.

Continuous improvement is embedded in OctoAcme's culture through structured retrospectives held after every sprint, release, or major milestone. Action items are tracked in the project backlog with clear owners and due dates, reviewed in weekly PM syncs, and measured for impact. This feedback loop, combined with standardized release and deployment practices (including staging smoke tests, rollback plans, and post-deploy verification), ensures that OctoAcme teams grow more effective with every iteration.
